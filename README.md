##Dear David,##
Most people facilitating AI workshops today picked up the tools in the last eighteen months. I came to them earlier — and from underneath.
By the time ChatGPT made AI a boardroom topic in late 2022, I had already been working with it, and I came to it from a decade in the technical stack beneath it: R, Python, Jupyter, corpus tools, computational analysis. I was building small training tools as far back as 2019. So when AI arrived, I was not learning it from scratch. I was recognising it. The wave is new to most people. It isn't to me.
What sets me apart is the combination. Plenty of people understand AI. Plenty of people can run a room. Far fewer do both well. Facilitation and instructional design are my profession, not a sideline — and that means I can take a group of nervous beginners or impatient executives and actually move them, rather than demonstrating the tool at them.
I have spent my career in regulated education environments, where everything has to survive audit and work for people who did not ask to be there. That is useful preparation for council and government rooms. I know how to make this material land for staff who are cautious, time-poor, and rightly sceptical — and how to keep a session practical rather than promotional.
I am comfortable across the full range you describe, from first-day AI literacy through to advanced prompting and power-user workflows, and across the platforms your clients actually use — Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. I also teach the one habit that makes adoption stick: knowing when to trust the output and when to check it. That single discipline is usually the difference between a team still using AI six months later and one that quietly dropped it.
